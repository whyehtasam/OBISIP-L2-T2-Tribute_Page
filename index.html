A visionary scientist


Born in Basra, Iraq, around the year 965, Al-Hasan Ibn al-Haytham, Latinised as Alhazen or Alhasen, was a pioneering scientific thinker who, from his observation of light entering a dark room, made major breakthroughs in understanding light and vision.

He set new standards in experimental science and completed his influential Book of Optics sometime around 1027.

Legacy


Ibn al-Haytham is widely considered to be the father of modern optics. He made significant contributions to the understanding of vision, reflection, refraction, and the nature of light. His work laid the foundation for many of the optical devices that we use today, such as eyeglasses, telescopes, and microscopes.

Al-Haytham's most influential work is his book Book of Optics, which he wrote in the early 11th century. In this book, he laid out his theory of vision, which was based on the idea that light rays travel from objects to the eye, rather than from the eye to objects. He also described the phenomenon of refraction, which is the bending of light rays as they pass from one medium to another.

Al-Haytham's work was translated into Latin in the 13th century, and it had a major influence on the development of optics in Europe. His work was cited by many of the great scientists of the Renaissance and Scientific Revolution, including Roger Bacon, Galileo Galilei, and Johannes Kepler.

Al-Haytham's legacy is not just limited to optics. He also made contributions to other fields of science, such as mathematics, astronomy, and physics. He was a true polymath, and his work had a profound impact on the development of science in the Islamic world and beyond.
